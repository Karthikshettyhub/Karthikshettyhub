## hey, i'm karthik shetty 

started out fascinated by how god made things — biology, nature, living systems and the quiet elegance behind all of it. somewhere along the way that curiosity shifted toward what humans build — and that's what pulled me into computer science.

---

## the journey starts :)

**getting the foundations right**
didn't want to just jump in blindly, so i started with harvard's CS50 — worked through it properly, understood how a computer actually thinks, how memory works, why certain decisions are made at the lowest level. that course changed how i see everything.

**understanding how real systems are built**
after the basics, things got interesting. i started exploring how data is actually organized inside systems — how the choice of structure changes the way you can query, traverse, or store things. how an algorithm isn't just a solution but a *decision* — one that determines whether your system handles ten users or ten million. the efficiency, the trade-offs, the reason certain approaches exist. that's what got me hooked to data structures and algo.

**going deeper into the backend**
from there it was a natural slide into backend and databases. started reading about how data-intensive applications are actually built at scale — what happens when your data grows beyond what a single machine can handle, how engineers have solved consistency, availability, and failure over decades. got into relational databases, understanding not just how to use them but *why* they're designed the way they are.

**a little bit of frontend too**
picked up some frontend along the way — because understanding the full picture matters. knowing what's happening on both ends makes you think better about the systems connecting them.

---

## what i'm into right now 

- backend systems & how they scale
- databases — relational models, queries, design
- understanding the engineering decisions behind real-world software
- frontend to see the full picture

## what's next 

the rabbit hole goes deeper. operating systems — because you can't truly understand the stack without knowing what's beneath it. and AI/ML after that, not because it's trending, but because the systems behind it — the infrastructure, the scale, the math — that's where things get genuinely interesting.
